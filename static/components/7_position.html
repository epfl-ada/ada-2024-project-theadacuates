Html Elements

Before proceeding, we present images illustrating the various HTML elements that may appear on a Wikipedia page. These elements play distinct roles in structuring content and can potentially influence player navigation choices:

  • Heading: Titles and subtitles that organize sections.
  • Paragraph: Main blocks of text containing information.
  • Table: Structured data or summaries, often used for comparative or statistical information.
  • List: Bulleted or numbered items, grouping related points or examples.
  • Infobox: Highlighted summary boxes, typically on the side, summarizing key information about the topic.
  • Footer: Content at the bottom, often with references or links to related articles.
  • Undefined: Elements that don’t fit into the standard categories listed above.

How Link Position is Quantified?

Since monitor resolution cannot be inferred from our dataset, we cannot assume it is the same for every player. Therefore, we are unable to determine the exact visual position of a link on a webpage a priori.

However, we decided to use the order of link appearance on the page as a metric for position.

Similarly, we assume that users exhibit no bias in their selection of links, and all links on a page have an equal probability of being clicked. If users have no bias toward link positions, the distribution of clicked links should resemble the distribution of all links on the page.

For instance, when analyzing the distribution of all link positions on a page, if there is only one link, we assign a weight of 1 to the link at position 1. If there are ten links, we assign equal weights of 0.1 to links in positions 1 through 10.

For a target link clicked by a user, if it appears in multiple positions on the page (e.g., positions 1 and 30), we consider the user to have clicked the link 0.5 times at position 1 and 0.5 times at position 30.

Kolmogorov Smirnov Test

The Kolmogorov Smirnov test (KS-Test) is used to compare two distributions to determine if they are pulling from the same underlying distribution.

Unlike other metrics that allow you to compare distributions such as JS divergence, PSI and KL divergence that come from mathematician Claude Shannon’s information theory, the KS-Test derives from statistics. It’s non-parametric, meaning it works across all types of distributions. Thus, it is not limited to use on well-known distributions such as normal or binomial – your data can take on any form, and the KS-Test still works.
